“The web dwells in a never-ending present. It is—elementally—ethereal, ephemeral, unstable, and unreliable.” - Jill Lepore
In the article, “The Cobweb: Can the Internet be archived?”, Lepore discusses the volatile nature of the web, identifying many ways in which information on the web is destroyed, such as link rot, overwritten data, and content drift. This was particularly surprising to me because I was under the impression that information was recorded indefinitely on the web. It was quite interesting to learn about methods some people are creating to combat the transience of the web, such as the creation of the Wayback Machine by Brewster Kahle.

To be truly countercultural today, in a time of tech hegemony, one has to, above all, betray the platform, which may come in the form of betraying or divesting from your public online self -Caroline Busta
I found this quote from the reading quite interesting as it is basically stating that in order to be countercultural, one must betray their own online presence. It is true that social media has gained a significant role in many of our lives, creating its own collective society. It has created a new set of expectations that many users succumb to in order to feel like they belong.

In a section of the reading, Chimero mentions the concept of spiritual technology and proceeds to examine a survey conducted by the Center for Humane Technology. In this survey, participants had their app usage tracked on their phones and were required to report whether they were happy with the time they spent on each app. The results revealed that the top apps participants regretted using the most were Grinder, Candy Crush Sage, Facebook, etc. It was suprising to find that the top three of the top four most regretted apps could be considered spiritual technology, which seems to defeat the whole purpose of spiritual technology.
